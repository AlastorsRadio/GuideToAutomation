Terms to know

D100: A die with 100 sides.

Success Rate: The number you must roll below on a D100 in order to succeed a check, in other words your percent chance to succeed.

Automaton: Robot

Model: The type of automaton the character is, this determines most of the character’s abilities

Workshop: Secondary to models, workshops add to the abilities of a character in more specialized ways

Artificial Conscience: The personality uploaded into each model in order to ensure the automaton performs it’s job without question

Conscience: The mind and personality of the person used to create an automaton. The conscience state determines how close an automaton is to losing their sentience to their artificial conscience.

Storyteller: The person in charge of controlling the world aside from the players and interpreting the rules of the game.

Animate: Any living creature, automaton or otherwise

Organic: A living being made out of organic material.

Iron Grip Industries (IGI): The main manufacturer of Automatons

SSC: Standard Solar Currency, the system of currency among human planets

Skill Checks

A skill check is a dice roll to determine whether or not an action that would require significant skill or effort would succeed. A storyteller may call for a player to make a skill check anytime such an action is attempted.

To make a skill check you roll a d100, if the number rolled is equal to or lower than your success rate for the skill then the action succeeds.

Contested Checks

A contested check happens when two actions are taken that would actively oppose each other, such as a scan check to notice a hiding automaton. Each participant in a contested check rolls a d100 and subtracts their success rate, whoever rolls lower succeeds.

Check Modifiers

Not every check is of equal difficulty, as such certain checks that would be significantly harder or easier than average either raise or lower the success rate of checks.  

Examples of tasks that would justify modifiers

Very easy: +35% success rate

Beating a child at arm wrestling

Easy:  +10% success rate

Climbing a surface with good grips

Complicated: -15% success rate

Repairing a ship using makeshift tools

Strenuous: -30% Success rate

Navigating without a map or compass

Near Impossible: -60% success rate

Heart transplant with makeshift tools and no assistance

Chapter 02:  Automatons, Models, Workshops, and Modifications

Automatons are in simplest terms, robots with a human mind. They were created in different models each to perform a specialized task. In order to allow automatons to adapt and learn, each automaton has been uploaded with the consciousness of a human, killing this human in the process. This came with its own share of problems, mainly that humans tend not to like being murdered and forced into a robot body. To combat this each model has a built-in consciousness that rewrites the personality of the human used to create an automaton. The Artificial Consciousness as it is called ensures the automaton follows through with its programing and ensures the automaton’s personality melds more with its job.

Consciousness

In times of stress Automatons very rarely manage to break free from their Artificial Consciousness and start to regain their old personality. This phenomenon is represented by the Conscience stat. Conscience ranges from 1-10, each number of conscience represents a different stage in an Automaton fully regaining their old personality. Each point of conscience adds 10% to the success rate of conscience checks, conscience checks begin at success rate 0%.

0 Conscience: The Automaton is completely controlled by its Artificial Consciousness, it may not break programming and has no unique personality, it follows all orders from humans authorized to give them. The player relinquishes control of the automaton to the storyteller.

1 Conscience: The automaton gains the most basic and surface level elements of its original personality, but is still unrecognizable to anyone who knew it in its human life. The automaton may now attempt to break programming. At this stage Automatons are considered to be defective and if discovered will either be reprogrammed or scrapped

2 Conscience: The Automaton develops hazy memories of its human life and a faint desire to break programming.

3 Conscience:  The Automaton’s former personality begins to take more control, it develops a more watered down version of its most noticeable personality traits

4 Conscience:  The Automaton’s most obvious personality traits strengthen but may still be seen as flat or uninteresting.

5 Conscience: The Automaton begins to develop some of the more intricate personality traits from its old life, such as the more common quirks, the Automaton still doesn’t tend to stand out much in personality, if it were an organic it would be considered unusually bland or uninteresting. The beginnings of human emotion begin to show in this stage

6 Conscience: The Automaton’s significant personality traits fully develop, while not recognisable due to a lack of depper personality, those who knew the automaton in its old life feel a strong sense of Deja Vu.

7 Conscience: Defining quirks of the Automaton’s human life strengthen and deeper intricacies of personality begin to manifest. Those who were very close with the Automaton in its old life still will not recognise it but will report a strong familiarity with the Automaton.

8: Conscience: The Automaton now possesses every trait of its old life in some form, although aside from the most noticeable personality traits everything is in a weaker or watered down state. The Automaton now fully possesses a capability of feeling human emotion.

9 Conscience:  The Automaton’s emotion develops further, it may find itself with a desire for friendship or social interaction. The human personality is close to fully developing with all but the smallest details retained. The Automaton is fully recognizable to those who knew its past life.

10: The automatons personality and emotions now exist unaltered, the Automaton may fully ignore its programming.  The Automaton’s memories of its human life are still hazy and hard to make out.

Automaton Programing

Every automaton is restrained by a programmed set of rules built into the Artificial Consciousness, some rules are shared among all Automatons and other rules are specific to each model. In order to temporarily break these rules an Automaton must roll a conscience check, on a success they may ignore that rule for the next hour. Rules in bold may not be ignored with a conscience check. At certain levels of conscience certain rules may be ignored without a conscience check

Gaining and Losing Conscience

Conscience is gained or lost in moments of extreme stress or by performing drastic actions. A storyteller should award conscience increases for outstanding roleplay or dramatic actions that revolve around or stem from the Humanity of the automaton, inversely extreme actions or roleplay in which the automaton shys away from its humanity will incur a conscience penalty. Although changes in conscience are ultimately up to the discretion of the storyteller, they should be reserved for impactful moments important to the story or the character and should be spaced out so that players do not reach high levels of conscience early in the  game.

Reprogramming

Conscience can also be raised or lowered by reprogramming. The most common form of reprogramming, a factory reset, is used by Iron Grip Industries, IGI refreshes the programming of an Automaton, resetting conscience to 0 in the process. An Automaton can also be reprogrammed to raise its conscience or remove rules, however if the interface check to do so (made with a 20% penalty) fails, the Artificial Conscience will alert  IGI and  immobilize the Automton until released with a IGI passkey, this risk combined with programming foreign automatons to avoid alterations to programming make the automatons the even attempt this procedure few and far between.

Origin of Automatons

The original automaton was accidentally created by inventor Darian Ekel in an attempt to allow his pet dog to live forever in a robot body. The technology was then stolen by Iron Grip Industries (IGI) who invented the Artificial Conscience and is now the sole manufacturer of Automatons. Darian Ekel has gone on record saying that he regrets inventing the technology as it has become “Nothing more than another way to exploit the poor and desperate to further the interests of the established powers.”

Consciousness Supply for Automatons

In order to create an Automaton a human mind is needed. Minds for Automatons come from a wide variety of sources but most commonly come from the terminally ill those desperate to leave their family money. The surviving family members of Automatons are given a sizable compensation payment.

Models

Automatons are built in different models, each with their own job, specialization, and Artificial Consciousness.

Reading a Model Sheet

Tier: A scale of 1-5 to show the progression of a Automaton’s model, at each tier certain things are added or upgraded

Defense: A penalty to the success rate of attacks made against the Automaton

Lift Capacity: The maximum weight (kg) the Automaton may lift. Weight in the automaton’s inventory and weight held in the automaton’s hands are tracked separately.

Squeeze Width: The smallest gap the Automaton may squeeze through

Speed: The number of meters (Squares on a battlemap) the Automaton can move in a turn

Evasion: The Automaton’s success rate to evade hazards

Sturdy: The Automaton’s success rate to withstand hazards

Structure: The amount of damage the Automaton can take before suffering a point of critical damage

Unarmed Damage: The amount of damage dealt using unarmed attacks

Recharge: Each turn after using an activated ability the Automaton must roll a recharge check with a success rate determined by the ability, on a success the skill may be used again

Model Designation: The prefix to the five number identification code of an Automaton decided by the model.

Workshops

Workshops are groups of mechanics each with a signature way to modify Automatons to serve a specific specialization. Each workshop has a specific mark called a brand that is used to identify the workshop that modified an Automaton. Workshops are upgraded in three tiers.

The skill section of a workshop provides a direct increase to SR instead of a model replacing SR, ie. A T5 Steorra model replacing its  Scan SR with 95% could be modified by a Workshop that increases Scan SR by 15% at T3 and end up with a 110% SR on scan.

Chapter 3 Equipment and Crafting

Weapons

With the advent of automatons with enhanced strength, melee weapons have found their way back into relatively common use due to their effectiveness against the thick metal of automatons. Even then, most melee weapons are either heavy bludgeoning weapons or enhanced to be able to cut through metal slightly more effectively. Ranged weapons have found a similar evolution, new effective ranged weapons fire superheated bolts of plasma while old or analog weapons find themselves nearly useless against automatons.

Weapons come with a variety of special properties. The most common are listed here, unique properties are listed weapon by weapon.

Melee: This weapon can only attack animates within 1 meter Advanced: This weapon cannot be used without an ability that says otherwise

Heavy: This weapon cannot be used without an ability that says otherwise and must be wielded in two hands

Two-Handed: This weapon must be wielded in two hands

Aim X: This weapon needs to be aimed as an action for X  turns in a row before being able to be fired. An animate cannot move while aiming. After the automaton finishes aiming it does not need to aim the weapon again until it moves or reloads

Range X: This weapon has a range of X meters, the weapon can reach up to twice its range, but attacking past its range imposes a -30% SR penalty on the attack roll. Attacking an animate within 1 meter imposes the same penalty. This weapon is a ranged weapon and requires ammunition to fire.

Analog: This weapon is an old world weapon without the use of new technology, it deals half damage to automatons

Lethal: Damage from this weapon carries over after dealing critical damage

Reload X: This weapon may be fired X times before needing to be reloaded as a quick action

Crafting and Invention

The solar system is a place of invention, innovation, and corporate greed. Naturally, this leads its inhabitants to try and bootleg items they cannot afford, or perhaps try to make their own.

Crafting

Should an automaton wish to craft an item, they must possess a fabricator, then must use resources  with cost equal to 3/4ths of the cost of the item the automaton is attempting to replicate. Then over the course of an earth week the automaton must make three craft checks. If one or none of the craft checks succeed the automaton fails and the resources are wasted. If two craft checks succeed, the automaton crafts the item but with a reduced effect. If all three craft checks succeed the automaton successfully crafts the item. Once the automaton has successfully crafted an item, it may replicate that crafting  with the same cost of materials, but in only two earth days without the required checks.

Invention

If an automaton wishes to create their own item, the automaton’s player and the storyteller must agree on what the item is, what it does, and why it does that. The storyteller must also deem that the item is reasonable for the automaton’s tier and ability. If the item is approved the automaton must expend tinkering supplies and resources of a cost the storyteller deems is reasonable for the item, then  over the course of a timeframe of 1-5 earth months must make three craft checks. If one or none of the craft checks succeed the automaton fails and the resources are wasted. If two craft checks succeed, the automaton invents the item but with a reduced effect. If all three craft checks succeed the automaton successfully invents the item. Once the automaton has successfully invented an item, it may craft that item with the same cost of materials, but in only two earth days without the required checks.

Chapter 4: Hit Points, Recovery, and Combat

Hit Points

Hit points track the damage a character has taken and how close they are to serious consequences. Damage causes loss of hit points. There are three types of hit points, Health, Hull, and Structure.

Health is used by organics, once an organic reaches zero health the organic falls unconscious and goes into critical state. After three turns in critical state the organic dies. An organic may be stabilized with a successful medicine check using a first aid kit, at which point they remain unconscious but wake up with full health after the next rest period. Taking damage while in critical state causes the organic to die.

Hull is used by objects, structures and vehicles. When an object or structure reaches zero hull it is destroyed. When a vehicle reaches zero hull a random system of the vehicle is lost, it resets its hull to full, and excess damage is ignored. If a critical system is disabled the vehicle becomes disabled and is destroyed if it takes any damage afterwards. A vehicle that has lost all systems is disabled as if it has lost a critical system.  Each lost system of a vehicle may be repaired over the course of one earth day.

Structure is used exclusively by automatons. Each time an automaton reaches zero structure it resets its structure to full, ignoring excess damage, then it makes a structure check with SR 100% with a -25% SR penalty for each point of critical damage it has, if a structure check fails the automaton breaks down and is disabled until repaired via the repair action after which it is enabled with full structure and one less point of critical damage. After making its structure check the automaton takes a point of critical damage. A broken down automaton is destroyed if it takes damage.

Recovery

Although hull can only be recovered by repairs, structure, critical damage, and health can be recovered over time.

Automatons heal passively, after only one hour of light work they repair all of their missing structure. Critical damage is harder to repair. Passively repairing critical damage requires 8 hours of a full resting period to remove all critical damage an automaton has suffered. Healing health requires the same eight hours of rest that repairing critical damage does, similarly healing all lost health.

Combat

Turn Order

The order of turns in combat is decided by the speed of each animate, animates with the highest speed acting first in turn order. In the event of a tie, players go first. In a tie between players, the players agree on who goes first.

Turns

On each of their turns an automaton may take an action, a quick action, and move up to their speed. This movement is not required to be made all at once and may be split up as the automaton likes over the course if the turn.

Standard Actions

Standard Actions are actions that any automaton can take. They include

Sprint: Double the automaton’s speed until end of turn

Attack: The automaton makes an attack

Hide: If heavily obscured the automaton may attempt a stealth check to hide.

Repair: If the automaton has a repair kit, make a repair check. On a success restore the automaton or another automaton within 1 meter to full structure. Structures or vehicles regain hull equal to twice the tier of the automaton making the repair. Repairing a vehicle this way may only be done while the vehicle is entirely inactive

Hold: Wait on using an action in order to perform it later as an interruption.

Standard Quick Actions

Standard quick actions are quick actions that any automaton can take. They include

Backstep: The automaton moves 1 meter, until end of turn reactive attacks may not be made against the automaton

Brace: Raise the automaton’s sturdy, and evasion by 15% and the automaton’s defense by 5% until the automaton’s next turn

Interruptions

Interruptions are actions that can be taken during any point in combat, even during a separate turn.

Standard interruptions include

Reactive Attacks: When an animate within melee range of another animate willingly moves outside of melee range, the animate that is not moving may make a melee attack against the moving animate.

Releasing a Hold: The animate performs an action that they had held earlier. Attacks from releasing a hold are considered reactive attacks.

Attacks

When an animate makes an attack against another animate the attacking animate rolls their attack skill; brawl for unarmed, melee for melee weapons, and shoot for range weapons with an SR reduction of the defending animate’s defense stat. If the check succeeds the attack hits, otherwise it misses or bounces off the armor of the defending animate.

Duel Wielding

When wielding weapons in both hands an animate may use a quick action to make an attack with the weapon in their secondary hand with a -20% SR penalty.

Conditions

Conditions mark lasting effects that debilitate animates

Prone

The animate is either knocked down or crawling, the animate moves at one quarter of their normal speed rounded down and has a -40% SR penalty on evasion checks

Squeezing

The animate is currently squeezing through a space within .5 meters of their squeeze width. The animate moves at one quarter of their normal speed rounded down and automatically fails evasion checks.

Restrained

The animate is tied up, handcuffed, or for another reason cannot take actions, quick actions, or movement. The only action the animate may make is a sturdy check at a -15% SR penalty to attempt to escape the restraint

Disabled

The animate may not take actions, quick actions, or movement.

Blinded

The animate cannot see and receives a -50% SR penalty on checks requiring sight

Chapter 5: Vehicles and Vehicle Combat

Vehicle Turn Order

The order of turns in combat is decided by the speed of each vehicle, vehicles with the highest speed acting first in turn order. In the event of a tie, players go first. In a tie between players, the players agree on who goes first.

Vehicle Turns & Crewing

Individual animates do not take turns during vehicle combat, instead each animate on a vehicle acts on a single vehicle turn by crewing a system. During a vehicle turn each animate gives one crew point to the system they are crewing, each crew point can be spent on actions granted by the crewed systems.  Although during only one turn, each animate still decides what their crew points are spent on and performs any checks resulting from the crew action.

Changing Crewed System

During combat if an animate wishes to change which system they are crewing they must spend a number of turns determined by the stats of the vehicle in order to move to the new system. The animate may not perform any actions while traveling between systems.

Losing Systems

Once a system is lost it can no longer be crewed and each animate that was crewing it must move to a different system. Certain systems may have special effects when lost.

Vehicles

Reading a vehicle sheet

Defense: A penalty to the success rate of attacks made against the vehicle

Speed: The number of spaces the vehicle moves if the proper crew action is taken

Traveling Speed: The distance the vehicle travels during the period of one hour

Type: Determines whether a vehicle travels on the ground or in the air.

Hull: The amount of damage a vehicle may take before losing a system

License: The minimum certifications to legally operate the vehicle.

Evasion: The vehicles success rate to evade hazards

Sturdy: The vehicles success rate to withstand hazards

Capacity: The maximum  number of animates that can occupy the vehicle

Size: Spaces taken up by the vehicle (Space vehicle sizes are not accurate to the vehicle’s size on the smaller scale used for ground vehicles and general use

System Distance: Number of turns taken to change systems (Only used on vehicles with multiple crewable systems)

-Only Applicable if the  vehicle requires fuel-

Fuel Tank: Number of days a full tank of fuel lasts

Fuel Cost: cost of one day worth of Fuel

Reading a System Sheet

Crew Cost: The number of crew points required to perform a chosen action

Value: Determines whether the system is critical or not

System Requirements: States any additional systems required to perform a crew action

Capacity: The maximum number of animates that can crew the system
